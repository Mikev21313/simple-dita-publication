Transmission Systems

Components and types of automotive power transmission systems.

Purpose and Function

A transmission is a system in a vehicle that transmits power from the engine or motor to the drive wheels, while providing torque conversion and speed control. The primary functions of a transmission include:

  • Multiplying torque at low speeds to overcome inertia and begin vehicle movement
  • Providing appropriate gear ratios for different driving conditions
  • Allowing the engine to operate in its optimal RPM range regardless of vehicle speed
  • Enabling reverse movement
  • Allowing the vehicle to idle while stopped (by disconnecting or neutralizing the drivetrain)

Manual Transmissions

Manual transmissions require driver operation of both the clutch and gear selection:

  • Components:
    • Clutch assembly (clutch disc, pressure plate, release bearing)
    • Input shaft connected to the engine via clutch
    • Output shaft connected to driveline
    • Gear sets (including synchronizers to match speeds)
    • Shift forks and linkage for gear selection
  • Operation: Driver depresses clutch pedal to disconnect engine from transmission, selects appropriate gear with shifter, then releases clutch to re-engage power flow
  • Advantages: Greater driver control, typically more fuel efficient, generally simpler and less expensive to repair, often more durable
  • Disadvantages: Requires more driver skill, can be tiring in stop-and-go traffic, potential for clutch wear

Automatic Transmissions

Automatic transmissions change gears without direct driver input:

  • Components:
    • Torque converter (replaces clutch in manual transmissions)
    • Planetary gear sets for different gear ratios
    • Hydraulic control system with valve body
    • Multiple clutch packs and bands to engage/disengage gear elements
    • Electronic control unit to manage shift points
  • Operation: Transmission automatically selects appropriate gear based on vehicle speed, engine load, and driver input. Torque converter allows vehicle to stop without stalling the engine.
  • Advantages: Easier to operate, better for stop-and-go traffic, modern versions can be highly efficient
  • Disadvantages: Traditionally less fuel-efficient, more complex with higher repair costs, power loss through torque converter

Continuously Variable Transmissions (CVT)

CVTs provide seamless ratio changes without distinct gear shifts:

  • Components:
    • Primary pulley connected to the engine
    • Secondary pulley connected to the drive wheels
    • Metal belt or chain connecting the pulleys
    • Hydraulic pressure system to vary pulley diameter
    • Electronic control unit
  • Operation: Rather than fixed gear ratios, CVTs use variable-diameter pulleys connected by a belt or chain to infinitely vary the gear ratio, keeping the engine at optimal RPM
  • Advantages: Optimal fuel economy, seamless acceleration, maintains ideal engine RPM
  • Disadvantages: Different driving feel (no gear shift sensation), traditionally limited torque capacity, can be noisy under heavy acceleration

Dual-Clutch Transmissions (DCT)

DCTs combine elements of manual and automatic transmissions:

  • Components:
    • Two separate clutches (one for even gears, one for odd gears)
    • Mechanically similar to manual transmissions but with automated operation
    • Electronic control unit and hydraulic or electromechanical actuators
  • Operation: One clutch engages the current gear while the other pre-selects the next anticipated gear, allowing for extremely fast shifts without interrupting power flow
  • Advantages: Lightning-fast shifts, excellent efficiency, direct power transfer without torque converter losses
  • Disadvantages: Can be jerky at low speeds, higher complexity and cost, heavier than manual transmissions

Automated Manual Transmissions (AMT)

AMTs are manual transmissions with automated clutch and shift operations:

  • Components:
    • Conventional manual transmission architecture
    • Electro-hydraulic or electro-mechanical actuators for clutch and shifting
    • Electronic control unit
  • Operation: Operates like a manual transmission but with computer-controlled clutch and shifting operations
  • Advantages: Lower cost than other automatics, good fuel efficiency, lightweight
  • Disadvantages: Shifts can be slower and less smooth than other automatic types

Electric Vehicle Transmissions

Electric vehicles often use simplified transmission systems:

  • Single-Speed Reduction Gear: Most common in EVs due to the wide torque band of electric motors
  • Multi-Speed Transmissions: Some higher-performance EVs use 2-3 speed transmissions for improved efficiency and performance
  • Advantages: Simplicity, reliability, no shifting required
  • Design Considerations: Must handle high torque at zero RPM, operate quietly, and maximize efficiency

Drivetrain Configurations

Transmissions deliver power to wheels in various arrangements:

  • Front-Wheel Drive (FWD): Engine and transmission in front, driving front wheels
  • Rear-Wheel Drive (RWD): Engine in front, transmission sends power through driveshaft to rear wheels
  • All-Wheel Drive (AWD): Power delivered to all four wheels, typically with emphasis on on-road performance
  • Four-Wheel Drive (4WD): Power can be delivered to all wheels with emphasis on off-road capability, often with selectable modes
  • Transaxle: Combined transmission and differential in one unit, common in FWD vehicles

Modern Trends

Recent developments in transmission technology include:

  • Increasing number of gear ratios (8-10 speeds) for better efficiency and performance
  • Hybrid transmissions integrating electric motors
  • Improved CVT designs with simulated shift points for more familiar driving feel
  • Shift-by-wire technology eliminating mechanical linkages
  • Advanced control algorithms for smoother shifts and better anticipation of driver needs
  • Integration with driver assistance systems and navigation to optimize shift patterns
